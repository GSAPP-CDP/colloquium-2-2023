Concept 1: Geospatial radio tuner:

Research question 1:

How could I design and build a digital instrument-computer that:

  1. Presents sound in cartesian space, and space sonically?
    1. Infrastructure (e.g., terrestrial radio, telecomms, flight paths)
    2. Landscape/terrain (digital elevation models, bathymetry, magnetic fields)
    3. Time? (time zones, time series dimension, history, habits)
  2. Organizes space in a way that rejects colonial cartographic archetypes? (e.g., continuous space versus discrete boundaries)
  3. Considers Minimal Computation to maximize affordability/accessibility and minimize prescriptiveness of usage? (Does this embrace web connectivity or reject it?)
  4. Rejects typical technological constructions of obsolescence?
  5. Enables user-performers to engage with a network of peers, either while operating the device or by contributing to its design and development?

I am interested in constructing a custom instrument-device that sonifies+graphs parameters exposed by the FCC open data portal + atmospheric, terrain, and other conditions local to each node in the vast physical network of US telecommunications. The instrument closes a recursive loop between space, sound, and transmission + integrates the performer/performance as a breakpoint.

To do this, I would design and fabricate a physical device (modeled loosely after an ‘etch-a-sketch’). The device allows a user to tune their device to “survey” a TBD spatial phenomenon (e.g, network of internet radio nodes.) Spatial phenomena and dimensions of infrastructure, landscape, and terrain are “mapped” to sonic dimensions.

Tune along the interpolated lines between nearest nodes; rescale the map using sliders on the X and Y axes of the central ’sketch’ screen, which in turn adjusts sonic responsiveness; switch between user-controlled scan/pan behavior and an auto-pan that creates an effect like finding yourself between stations on a road trip.

The instrument will be a standalone 3D printed or CNC cut physical interface with a simple (eg 1-bit memory/OLED) display that enables adjustment of the application’s parameters with encoders and sliders. An affordable (~$15) raspberry pi zero should be sufficient to compute graphics and streaming audio concurrently, though it has more demanding power requirements and needs to boot up before use. Concept 2: Generalizable methods of spatial data sonification:

Research question 2:

How can we hear a map? What are the creative+practical uses of cross-sensory thinking, orientation, and navigation? How can multi-sensory interpretations of space and place alter a person’s cognition and perception? Are there ramifications on attention and memory? Identity?

The physical properties of sound are widely understood, measurable, and consequently can be modeled reasonably well. For example, Noisetools.net features a browser-based Decibel Map (https://noisetools.net/dbmap/), a multidimensional analysis of sound transmission (i.e., loudness) across physical space. The model parameterizes terrain, ground type (factor), barrier construction, sound reflections, and atmospheric conditions. There are several assumptions made, including sound source directionality, wind speed + direction, ground type gradients, and reverberant fields. These qualities are also eminently modelable, if desired.

The phenomenological properties of sound - the human subjective and intersubjective experiences of sound - are less discrete and therefore would present an interesting dimension to a spatial data sonification exercise. E.g., exploring the “outer boundary” limits of human perception.

Concept 3: Terrestrial+digital radio:

Research question 3:

How can we bridge the divide between analog terrestrial radio infrastructure and intangible digital streaming infrastructures, specifically as they relate to discovery?

In the context of popular music (in contrast to "classical," which typically falls into formal institutional channels), the act of discovery has meaningful cultural, social, cultural, and financial consequences as artists and listeners alike rely on centralized platforms for transmission. Whereas conventional radio and its listeners are intractably linked to a geographic center, streaming platforms typically prompt the discovery of music and speech through opaque, aspatial metadata-based systems of recommendation.

This project could attempt to reclaim (or reconceptualize) the deeply geographic and spatial methods in which an artist or musician practices and shares their craft.

Some related questions:

  • how far does the internet go? How deep? How high? Re: digital network analogues to analog network transmissions
  • *where* are genre linkages in the post-streaming/file sharing age
  • ...how can we think about +visualize the linkages?
